# Research Paper Summaries — 2026-05-24

## quant-ph

This collection of research papers explores various aspects of quantum systems, non-Abelian gauge theories, and quantum information processing. A common theme among these papers is the investigation of quantum phenomena, such as entanglement, non-adiabaticity, and quantum control, in different contexts, including open systems, quantum circuits, and quantum communication. Many of these papers employ novel theoretical frameworks, such as bottom-up open effective field theories, geometric frameworks, and dynamic Pauli constraints, to analyze and understand complex quantum behaviors. The research also highlights the importance of considering environmental effects, noise, and non-adiabaticity in the design and optimization of quantum systems, including quantum circuits, quantum communication channels, and quantum heat engines. Overall, these papers contribute to the development of new theoretical tools and methodologies for understanding and controlling quantum systems, with potential applications in quantum computing, quantum communication, and quantum simulation.

### Bottom-up open EFT for non-Abelian gauge theory with dynamical color environment

**Authors:** Yoshihiko Abe, Kanji Nishii

**Summary:** This paper develops a bottom-up open effective field theory (EFT) for non-Abelian gauge theories, which describes the interactions between a system and its environment in a gauge-covariant manner. The framework provides a local open-EFT description of color transport, memory effects, and fluctuation-dissipation structure in non-Abelian plasmas. This approach offers a systematic starting point for dissipative Yang--Mills EFTs with dynamical environments.

[Link](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22822v1)

### How many systems can be dephased before the quantum switch becomes causally definite?

**Authors:** Yassine Benhaj, Kuntal Sengupta, Cyril Branciard

**Summary:** This research investigates how many systems can be dephased before a quantum process loses its causal nonseparability, and finds that dephasing all systems or keeping only the future system undephased renders the process causally separable. However, if any non-future system remains undephased, the process can retain causal nonseparability. This behavior is observed in both bipartite and multipartite quantum circuits with quantum control.

[Link](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22807v1)

### One-photon communication in atomic media

**Authors:** Zixiang Hong, John C. Schotland

**Summary:** This paper studies the transmission of single photons through atomic media and finds that the fidelity of the quantum channel decreases monotonically with coupling strength, establishing a performance bound for quantum communication. The results apply to various channel types and both deterministic and random media, providing a fundamental limit on the reliability of quantum communication through atomic media.

[Link](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22797v1)

### Geometric Origin of the Non-Adiabaticity Parameter and Self-Limiting Instability in Driven Nonlinear Systems

**Authors:** A. M. Tishin

**Summary:** This paper introduces a geometric framework to understand non-adiabaticity in driven nonlinear systems, providing a local criterion to evaluate instability and its suppression. The framework reveals that an occupation-dependent regulator can limit the evolution speed, leading to bounded dynamics. This approach offers a compact criterion for self-limited non-adiabatic instability in driven nonlinear bosonic systems.

[Link](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22796v1)

### Adiabatic Quantum Phase Estimation

**Authors:** Alexander Schmidhuber, Seth Lloyd

**Summary:** The paper presents a simple adiabatic protocol for quantum phase estimation, achieving optimal Heisenberg-limited scaling in both precision and failure probability. This approach encodes eigenvalues in populations of computational basis states, making it robust against certain dephasing errors. The protocol requires only basic couplings and is suitable for analog hardware.

[Link](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22770v1)

### A sharp interaction-degree threshold for simulating QAOA

**Authors:** Ralfs Āboliņš, Andris Ambainis

**Summary:** This research identifies a sharp interaction-degree threshold for classically simulating the Quantum Approximate Optimization Algorithm (QAOA) with 2-local cost functions. The study shows that classical sampling from QAOA is feasible at degree 2 but becomes intractable at degree 3, where it would imply a collapse of the polynomial hierarchy. However, the hardness of sampling does not directly imply a quantum optimization advantage due to the trivial optimizability of the cost functions.

[Link](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22758v1)

### Quantum circuit design via dynamic Pauli constraints

**Authors:** James R. Wootton, Merlin Incerti-Medici, Daniel Bultrini, Pierre Fromholz

**Summary:** This paper introduces a novel software-oriented model of quantum computation that uses dynamic Pauli constraints to specify gates, allowing for a more practical and efficient approach to quantum circuit design. The model is proven to be equivalent to the coupling-graph-restricted circuit model and is universal for BQP with a polynomial overhead. This approach provides a natural interface for designing quantum software in terms of physically observable quantities, making it relevant for near-term quantum hardware.

[Link](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22744v1)

### Self-testing of exact entanglement embezzlement

**Authors:** Samuel J. Harris

**Summary:** This research paper explores the concept of exact entanglement embezzlement with a catalyst state vector and unitaries, and shows that any such protocol must arise from a unique state on the tensor product of the Cuntz algebra with itself. The paper proves that exact entanglement embezzlement is a self-test for a collection of Cuntz isometries and a unique quasi-free state on the Cuntz algebra. This result has implications for the study of entanglement and quantum information processing.

[Link](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22713v1)

### Sudden death of entanglement, rebirth of magic

**Authors:** Chenfeng Cao

**Summary:** This paper investigates the phenomenon of "sudden death of entanglement" and the "rebirth of magic" in quantum systems subject to local Markovian noise. The research shows that while entanglement cannot be restored by local noise, "magic" (a measure of quantum non-stabilizerness) can be regained, and that there is a complementary relationship between entanglement and magic. The paper also explores the implications of this phenomenon for magic-state distillation and the classification of pure stabilizer states into "magic-generators" and "magic-insulators".

[Link](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22603v1)

### Optimal work extraction in measurement-based quantum Otto engines: Non-adiabaticity and generalized measurements can be beneficial

**Authors:** Arunabha Das, Sayan Mondal, Debarupa Saha, Ujjwal Sen

**Summary:** This research paper explores the concept of measurement-based quantum heat engines, which use quantum measurements instead of a hot thermal reservoir, offering greater controllability and simplicity. The study investigates the optimal work extractable from a measurement-driven quantum Otto engine and finds that it can outperform conventional quantum Otto engines under certain conditions. The paper also shows that non-adiabatic implementations and the use of positive operator-valued measurements (POVMs) can lead to higher work output and efficiency than traditional methods.

[Link](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22583v1)

## cond-mat.stat-mech

This collection of research papers explores complex systems and phenomena across various disciplines, including statistical mechanics, quantum physics, network science, and artificial intelligence. A common thread among these studies is the use of theoretical models and analytical techniques to understand and predict the behavior of complex systems, often characterized by non-equilibrium dynamics, phase transitions, and emergent properties. Many of these papers employ methods from statistical physics, such as spin glass theory, percolation, and mean-field analysis, to study systems with interacting components, directional networks, and non-reciprocal interactions. The research also highlights the importance of understanding the interplay between disorder, correlations, and non-equilibrium processes in shaping the behavior of complex systems, with implications for fields ranging from quantum computing and materials science to social dynamics and machine learning. Overall, these studies contribute to a deeper understanding of the fundamental principles governing complex systems and their potential applications in various fields.

### Spin Glass Mapping of the Parallel Minority Game

**Authors:** Aryan Tyagi, Soumyaditya Das, Soumyajyoti Biswas, Anirban Chakraborti

**Summary:** The parallel minority game (PMG) is a complex optimization problem that can be mapped to an Ising spin glass model, revealing inherent frustration and connections to spin glass theory. This mapping allows for a new perspective on the frozen, sub-optimal configurations observed in stochastic strategies. By encoding agent choices as spin variables, the population variance becomes a quadratic Hamiltonian with random couplings and fields.

[Link](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22764v1)

### Posterior Collapse as Automatic Spectral Pruning

**Authors:** Johannes Hirn

**Summary:** Posterior collapse in β-VAEs is shown to implement automatic spectral pruning, where latent modes collapse if their contribution to reconstruction is below a certain cutoff. The collapse of latent modes follows a mean-field law and can be predicted using a Landau stability analysis. The authors derive an order parameter that ranks active latent modes and identifies which variables to inspect first.

[Link](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22691v1)

### Directed extended-range percolation

**Authors:** Wenbo Liu, Yiwen Zeng, Xueming Liu, Ginestra Bianconi

**Summary:** Directed extended-range percolation (DERP) is introduced as a framework for understanding percolation in directed networks with non-reciprocal edges. The authors show that directionality can reduce the complexity of percolation and enable an exact determination of the percolation threshold and critical indices on locally tree-like structures. Monte Carlo simulations corroborate the analytical predictions, highlighting the impact of directionality and correlations on long-range connectivity in complex networks.

[Link](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22646v1)

### Entanglement Dynamics across a Monitored Quantum Point Contact

**Authors:** Anna Delmonte, Marco Schirò

**Summary:** This paper explores the entanglement dynamics across a monitored quantum point contact, where particle losses are recorded, and demonstrates how local monitoring reshapes entanglement production. The results show a linear growth in entanglement entropy, followed by a slow decay, which is captured using a quasiparticle picture. The study connects its findings to the Page curve and experimentally relevant probes, with potential applications in mesoscopic systems and ultracold atoms.

[Link](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22555v1)

### The cell fluid model with Curie-Weiss interactions: special cases and analytical results

**Authors:** O. A. Dobush, M. P. Kozlovskii, I. V. Pylyuk, R. V. Romanik, M. A. Shpot

**Summary:** The paper analytically studies a cell fluid model with Curie-Weiss interactions, focusing on special cases such as the ideal-gas limit and the strong-repulsion limit. The authors derive explicit expressions for critical point parameters, the equation of state, and binodal and spinodal curves, and show that the equation of state agrees with that of the van der Waals lattice gas. The study also extends the validity range of the cell model to include the marginal case of thermodynamic stability.

[Link](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22520v1)

### Long-range nonstabilizerness of topologically encoded states from mutual information

**Authors:** David Aram Korbany, Tyler D. Ellison, David T. Stephen, Lorenzo Piroli

**Summary:** This research investigates long-range nonstabilizerness (LRN) in topologically encoded states using mutual information as a diagnostic tool. The study focuses on two-dimensional topologically-ordered systems, such as the toric code and the non-abelian string-net model, and analyzes the mutual information between non-overlapping regions. The results provide a full classification of LRN for encoded non-stabilizer states in the toric code, but not for the non-abelian string-net model, and constrain the logical gates that can be implemented fault-tolerantly on the torus.

[Link](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22424v1)

### Aggregation-Fragmentation Processes with Broken Detailed Balance

**Authors:** P. L. Krapivsky

**Summary:** This paper studies aggregation-fragmentation processes, where clusters can aggregate and break into fragments, and explores the conditions under which detailed balance holds. The authors find that when fragmentation rates scale with mass, detailed balance only holds when the scaling exponent is 1, and otherwise, the system exhibits non-equilibrium steady states. They also identify a shattering transition with continuous mass loss when the scaling exponent is negative.

[Link](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22383v1)

### Berezinskii-Kosterlitz-Thouless-type Transition in Site Percolation on the Diamond Hierarchical Lattice

**Authors:** Takehisa Hasegawa, Kazuki Wataya, Tomoaki Nogawa

**Summary:** This paper investigates site percolation on the diamond hierarchical lattice, a fractal network, and finds that the system exhibits a nonpercolating phase and a critical phase, but not a percolating phase. The critical phase is characterized by a subextensive largest cluster size and a correlation length that exhibits a Berezinskii-Kosterlitz-Thouless-type essential singularity. The authors argue that the critical phase emerges due to site dilution remaining relevant under renormalization.

[Link](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22103v1)

### Thermodynamic Irreversibility of Training Algorithms

**Authors:** Liu Ziyin, Yuanjie Ren, Adam Levine, Isaac Chuang

**Summary:** This paper establishes a framework for analyzing the irreversibility of training algorithms in AI systems, which introduce far-from-equilibrium dynamical processes. The authors show that different measures of irreversibility are equivalent and give rise to a time-reversal-symmetry-breaking emergent force that breaks non-isometric symmetries and leads to a preference for learning trajectories that minimize entropy production rate. This work provides a fundamental understanding of the learning dynamics of modern AI systems.

[Link](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.21933v1)

### A Utility-Driven Bounded-Confidence Model for Opinion Dynamics

**Authors:** Alex Siebenmorgen, Juan G. Restrepo

**Summary:** This research paper introduces a utility-driven bounded-confidence model for opinion dynamics, where opinions with higher utility have a stronger social influence. The model is analyzed using a stochastic differential equation, which shows that the mean opinion has a Gibbs-like stationary distribution determined by the utility landscape. The dynamics exhibit metastability and spontaneous switching between competing opinion states, especially for multimodal utility functions.

[Link](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.21844v1)

## physics.flu-dyn

This collection of research papers encompasses a diverse range of topics in fluid dynamics, aerodynamics, and turbulence, with a shared focus on understanding complex flow phenomena and developing novel methods for simulation and analysis. Many of the studies employ numerical simulations, experiments, or a combination of both to investigate various aspects of fluid behavior, such as gravity wave activity, magnetosonic wave turbulence, and turbulent convection. Common themes include the characterization of flow regimes, the identification of coherent structures, and the development of new modeling approaches, including lattice Boltzmann methods, wave-particle methods, and finite element models. The papers also highlight the importance of considering multiscale phenomena, non-uniformities, and curvature effects in fluid flows, with applications ranging from astrophysical plasmas and atmospheric science to biomedical engineering and geophysical flows. Overall, the research direction is geared towards advancing our understanding of complex fluid dynamics and developing more accurate and efficient simulation tools for predicting and analyzing various flow phenomena.

### Variation of Venusian Gravity Wave Absolute Momentum Fluxes and Drag as Retrieved from the Akatsuki Mission

**Authors:** Erdal Yiğit, Emilia Sloan

**Summary:** This study uses data from the Akatsuki mission to characterize gravity wave activity in the Venusian middle atmosphere, estimating absolute horizontal momentum fluxes and wave drag. The results show that momentum flux increases exponentially with altitude before peaking and attenuating, while wave drag becomes prominent where momentum flux decreases. These findings provide observational constraints on gravity wave momentum transport and dissipation in the Venusian atmosphere.

[Link](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22793v1)

### Dynamics of fast magnetosonic wave turbulence

**Authors:** Nicolás Pablo Müller, Sébastien Galtier

**Summary:** This research investigates the dynamics of fast magnetosonic wave turbulence through numerical simulations of the wave turbulence kinetic equation. The simulations reveal a mixture of forward and backward energy cascades, with the former being stronger, and confirm the existence of a Kolmogorov-Zakharov energy spectrum. The study provides a theoretical explanation for observations in the solar wind plasma and sheds light on the behavior of fast magnetosonic waves in astrophysical plasas.

[Link](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22710v1)

### Experimental investigation of twin pulsed jets in a hemispheric elastic cavity

**Authors:** L. S. Merlo, L. Kadem, W. Saleh, H. D. Ng, G. Di Labbio

**Summary:** This experimental study examines the fluid dynamics of twin pulsed jets in a hemispheric elastic cavity, varying the spacing and strength of the jets. The results reveal three distinct flow regimes and uncover rare aspects of twin vortex ring behavior, including symmetry breaking and wall-induced rebound mechanisms. The findings have direct relevance to cardiac fluid dynamics in both healthy and pathological conditions, particularly in the context of cardiovascular flows and medical interventions.

[Link](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22648v1)

### Perpendicular rod-airfoil aeroacoustics: experiments and modelling of interaction noise

**Authors:** Marios I. Spiropoulos, Filipe R. Amaral, Florent Margnat, Vincent Valeau, Peter Jordan

**Summary:** This paper investigates the aeroacoustic interaction between a cylinder and an airfoil, simulating the noise generated by an aircraft's landing gear. The study combines experimental measurements with modeling approaches, including Powell-Howe vortex-sound theory, to estimate the acoustic field and identify coherent structures in the flow field. The results show a reasonable agreement between the sound field estimations and measurements, highlighting the three-dimensional nature of the problem.

[Link](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22546v1)

### Lagrangian single-particle, multi-particle and topological analyses in turbulent Rayleigh-Bénard convection

**Authors:** Matti Ettel, Roshan J. Samuel, Michael Chertkov, Jörg Schumacher

**Summary:** This research presents a Lagrangian analysis of turbulent Rayleigh-Bénard convection, using direct numerical simulations to characterize convective transport along material trajectories. The study reveals extremely intermittent Lagrangian heat transfer and identifies a distinct topological footprint of convective vortices, providing mechanism-resolving diagnostics for active-scalar turbulence. The results also show that buoyancy- and shear-driven dispersion are temporally organized, with rapid plume-driven ejection followed by sustained Richardson-like scaling.

[Link](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22326v1)

### A unified gas-kinetic wave-particle method for multiscale binary-species gas mixtures

**Authors:** Junzhe Cao, Yufeng Wei, Wenpei Long, Chengwen Zhong, Kun Xu

**Summary:** This paper introduces a unified gas-kinetic wave-particle method for simulating multiscale binary-species gas mixtures, which enables the automatic decomposition of the gas distribution function into analytical hydrodynamic waves and discrete particles. The method is shown to accurately capture the differences in velocity and temperature between different species, and its predictions agree well with DSMC results for hypersonic flows. The approach offers significant advantages for simulating various multiscale physical phenomena, including hypersonic flows, plasma transport, and radiation transport.

[Link](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22274v1)

### N-Component Free Energy Lattice Boltzmann Method with Reduction Consistency and Global Momentum Conservation

**Authors:** Michael Rennick, Xitong Zhang, Tim Niklas Bingert, Mathias J. Krause, Halim Kusumaatmaja

**Summary:** This paper presents a free energy lattice Boltzmann model that can simulate fluid systems with multiple immiscible components, ensuring reduction consistency and global momentum conservation. The method is validated through various static and dynamic problems, demonstrating excellent agreement with theoretical predictions. The proposed method has potential applications in patterned liquid surfaces and microfluidic emulsion droplet generation.

[Link](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22214v1)

### Global exponential stability for the three-dimensional Navier-Stokes equations on hyperbolic space

**Authors:** Zhi-Wei Wang, Samuel L. Braunstein

**Summary:** This paper proves the global exponential stability of the three-dimensional Navier-Stokes equations on hyperbolic space, showing that solutions decay exponentially to zero for sufficiently small initial data. The exponential decay rate is determined by the dynamic viscosity and the spectral gap of the deformation Laplacian. The results demonstrate the impact of negative curvature on the stability of the Navier-Stokes equations.

[Link](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22212v1)

### Full Turbulence Simulation of Channel Flow at $Re_τ \approx 1000$

**Authors:** Yoshinobu Yamamoto, Yoshiyuki Tsuji

**Summary:** This paper presents a Full Turbulence Simulation of turbulent channel flow at a high Reynolds number, resolving the Kolmogorov wavenumber in all spatial directions. The simulation provides a reference dataset for the turbulence and dissipation characteristics of the intermediate layer, and identifies resolution criteria for accurate simulations. The results demonstrate the efficiency of spectral methods in reproducing turbulence statistics with high accuracy, and provide guidelines for high-Reynolds-number DNS simulations.

[Link](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22107v1)

### Modelling hydroelastic flexure of arbitrarily shaped ice shelves forced by long ocean waves

**Authors:** T. K. Papathanasiou, L. G. Bennetts, M. H. Meylan

**Summary:** This research paper presents a mathematical model to study the flexure of Antarctic ice shelves under the influence of long ocean waves, which can contribute to calving events. The model uses finite elements and a Dirichlet-to-Neumann map to simulate the behavior of ice shelves with arbitrary shapes and non-uniform thickness. The method is verified and used to study how ice shelf shape, wave direction, and grounding proportion affect ice shelf deflection, identifying resonant responses over a broad frequency range.

[Link](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22042v1)

## physics.ao-ph

This collection of research papers showcases a diverse range of topics in the fields of atmospheric science, climate modeling, and fluid dynamics, with a shared emphasis on advancing our understanding of complex systems and improving predictive capabilities. Many of the papers explore the intersection of physics-based and machine learning approaches, highlighting the potential benefits of blending these methods to enhance model performance and accelerate scientific discovery. Common themes include the development of new methodologies and frameworks for analyzing and simulating complex phenomena, such as ensemble spread in chaotic systems, typhoon sensitivity, and fluid flow. The papers also demonstrate a focus on evaluating and improving model performance, whether through the use of stochastic parameterizations, high-precision AI forecasting systems, or novel approaches to data analysis and visualization. Overall, these studies contribute to a deeper understanding of the underlying dynamics of complex systems and pave the way for the development of more accurate and reliable predictive models.

### Decomposing Ensemble Spread in Lorenz '96 With Learned Stochastic Parameterizations

**Authors:** Birgit Kühbacher, Daan Crommelin, Niki Kilbertus

**Summary:** This paper explores the decomposition of ensemble spread in the Lorenz '96 system, a widely used testbed for chaotic dynamics, to better understand the sources of uncertainty in weather and climate forecasts. The authors find that stochastic parameterizations, particularly those with temporally persistent structure, can enhance early spread growth and improve spread-error consistency. This research provides guidance for the design and evaluation of stochastic parameterizations in weather and climate models.

[Link](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22242v1)

### Water vapor buoyancy and the African easterly jet

**Authors:** Heng Quan, Da Yang, William Boos, Tiffany Shaw, Huazhi Ge, Yaoxuan Zeng, Carly KleinStern

**Summary:** This study examines the role of water vapor buoyancy in the African easterly jet, a prominent circulation feature in the tropical atmosphere, and finds that it substantially counteracts the effect of the temperature gradient on density. The authors show that this effect strengthens under global warming, highlighting the importance of moisture distribution on large-scale circulation. They also raise concerns about the relevance of climate change projections from models that do not include vapor buoyancy in their governing equations.

[Link](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.21875v1)

### A Simulation Methodology Testbed for Typhoon Sensitivity Analysis: Framework Development and Perturbation-Response Experiments with the Pangu Weather Model

**Authors:** Yuehua Peng, Yuchen Zhang, Qin Huang, Chengzhi Ye, Jingsong Yang

**Summary:** This paper develops a simulation methodology testbed for typhoon sensitivity analysis, integrating a high-precision AI forecasting system with closed-loop techniques to explore the response of typhoons to localized perturbations in their environmental fields. The testbed is used to perform controlled perturbation-response experiments, revealing the feasible perturbation-response range and input-output coupling relationships of the system. The study provides a framework for investigating typhoon sensitivity to artificial environmental perturbations and offers a foundation for subsequent expansion toward more complex analysis strategies.

[Link](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.21864v1)

### Microwave-Stimulated Serpentinization of Olivine for Geological Hydrogen Production

**Authors:** Ansan Pokharel, Terence Musho

**Summary:** This paper explores the use of microwave stimulation to accelerate the serpentinization of olivine, a process that can generate molecular hydrogen. The experiment showed a 12-fold increase in hydrogen concentration and a significant increase in reaction rate compared to conventional heating. The results suggest that microwave stimulation can enhance serpentinization kinetics, providing a potential route to accelerated geologic hydrogen production.

[Link](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.21790v1)

### Three-Dimensional Velocity Analysis and Particle Size Dynamics from Multi-Site RGB-Photometry of Noctilucent Clouds

**Authors:** Oleg S. Ugolnikov, Olga Yu. Golubeva, Egor O. Ugolnikov

**Summary:** A new method has been developed to measure the altitude, particle size, and velocity of noctilucent clouds using multi-site RGB-photometry. The method was applied to observational data from 2023-2025, revealing that meridional motion is the primary driver of changes in particle size. The study also examined the effect of particle size evolution in the presence of a strong latitudinal temperature gradient.

[Link](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.21687v1)

### From Licensing to Open Access: Designing a Sustainable Transition in Operational Weather Data

**Authors:** Emma Pidduck, Umberto Modigliani, Victoria L. Bennett, Fabio Venuti, Florian Pappenberger, Florence Rabier

**Summary:** The European Centre for Medium-Range Weather Forecasts (ECMWF) transitioned from a restricted data licensing model to open access, making operational weather data available under CC BY 4.0. The transition was implemented through a tiered service model, which allowed for the provision of core forecast data while offering supported delivery as a cost-recovered service. The results show that over 93% of previously paying organizations retained a service agreement, and open endpoint download volumes increased substantially, suggesting that a tiered service model can reconcile open-access obligations with operational sustainability.

[Link](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.21673v1)

### Effect of grid anisotropy, resolution, and subgrid-scale models in pseudo-spectral Large Eddy Simulations of low-level clouds

**Authors:** Davide Selvatici, Richard J. A. M. Stevens

**Summary:** This paper investigates the effects of grid resolution and subgrid-scale models on large-eddy simulations of low-level clouds, finding that a novel framework combining pseudo-spectral advection with the anisotropic minimum dissipation model produces accurate predictions across varying grid resolutions. The study recommends a grid anisotropy with vertical spacing approximately three times finer than horizontal spacing for a balance of accuracy and computational efficiency. This approach shows good agreement with theoretical predictions and improves convergence rates.

[Link](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.21196v1)

### Beyond Vorticity: An Angular Momentum Perspective on Fluid Flow

**Authors:** Ahmed Farooq

**Summary:** This paper introduces a new framework for analyzing fluid flow based on the angular momentum density field, which provides a complementary perspective to traditional vorticity-based approaches. The angular momentum perspective offers several theoretical advantages, including a novel decomposition of viscous torque and a unified budget for non-circulatory added mass and circulatory lift. This approach also simplifies geophysical fluid dynamics and enables the direct calculation of viscous added mass force.

[Link](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.21191v1)
